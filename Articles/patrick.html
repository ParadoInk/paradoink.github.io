We Need a 32 Hour Work Week.

By
GreenMachine11713 and UnorthodoxAmbassador

It is long overdue: America needs a 32 hour work week with no reduction in pay.

It is long overdue: America needs a 32 hour work week with no reduction in pay. Even with massively increased productivity due to automation, those who work have seen no reduction in their hours. In fact, the automated systems which now run many jobs have intensified the stress placed on workers. Why does this keep happening? If only someone had written about this 150 years ago…

If machinery be the most powerful means for increasing the productiveness of labor — i.e., for shortening the working-time required in the production of a commodity, it becomes in the hands of capital the most powerful means, in those industries first invaded by it, for lengthening the working-day beyond all bounds set by human nature… -Karl Marx, ‘Capital’

As a largely de-industrialized nation, some would argue that the traditional relationship between mechanization and employment has largely been settled. The millions of manufacturing workers have already lost their jobs, “America isn’t a country that produces anymore” one might think.

In an extremely literal sense, this may be true - we produce far less manufactured goods than 20, 30, or 40 years ago. Some would argue Walmart surpassing General Motors as the nation's largest employer in the early 2000s marked the end of manufacturing. Instead, I see our shift as from manufacturing goods to manufacturing services. Once we realize that the fundamental nature of production has not changed, but merely its aesthetics, it becomes clear how Marx’s observations in Capital are still highly relevant.

Workers now are fundamentally no different than 100 years ago - and the same antagonism remains. If left unchecked, the capitalists use automation to displace workers and intensify the work for those who remain, extracting as much labor from them as possible. This can be seen all across America’s new service economy, The development of newer and more efficient machines have seen workers be further reduced to mere cogs in the machine of capital. In Amazon packing plants, workers work brutal 12-hour days where the pain is so normalized that medications are sold in company vending machines. Having worked grueling and long manual labor jobs myself, I feel this pain as my own. In my case, however, I did not work for a massive company like Amazon, which uses its cutting-edge technology to push workers far past their physical limits. When the regulation of labor loosens, people suffer. When I worked on manual labor, I suffered numerous repetitive use injuries in my hands and hyperextensions in my arms and legs. Humans are not built to work these jobs for such long hours - it breaks people.

And so, we return to the question of working hours. With the increased intensity, stress, and efficiency of jobs now, it only makes sense to reduce the number of working hours - anything less is to condemn workers to toil in increasingly harsh and dangerous conditions. There could only ever be a good outcome from a societal shift like this. Workers would have more time doing things they otherwise wouldn't have time for; exercise, spending time with their children, going shopping. In the United Kingdom, some companies have already switched to the 4 day work week and have actually reported a surge in productivity and employee morale.
